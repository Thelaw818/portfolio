Color Theory Overview

Color Theory Overview

Color theory is the study of how colors interact and how they affect human perception and emotion. It is a framework that helps designers, artists, and creators make deliberate and informed choices about colors to achieve desired effects in their work. Understanding the relationships between colors, the psychology of colors, and how colors can be combined is essential for creating visually compelling designs.

Key Color Theory Terms

Color Wheel
A circular diagram that shows the relationships between colors.
Primary Colors
Red, blue, and yellow; the base colors from which other colors are mixed.
Secondary Colors
Colors formed by mixing two primary colors (e.g., green, orange, and purple).
Tertiary Colors
Colors formed by mixing a primary color with a secondary color (e.g., red-orange, yellow-green).
Complementary Colors
Colors opposite each other on the color wheel, creating high contrast.
Analogous Colors
Colors next to each other on the color wheel that create harmony.
Hue
The pure color without tint, shade, or tone.
Tint
A color mixed with white, making it lighter.
Shade
A color mixed with black, making it darker.
Tone
A color mixed with gray, making it less intense.
Saturation
The intensity or purity of a color.
Brightness
The perceived lightness or darkness of a color.
Warm Colors
Colors such as red, orange, and yellow that evoke warmth.
Cool Colors
Colors such as blue, green, and purple that evoke calmness.
Monochromatic
A color scheme using variations of a single hue.
Triadic Colors
Three colors evenly spaced around the color wheel.
Split-Complementary
A color scheme using a base color and two colors adjacent to its complement.
RGB
A color model using red, green, and blue, commonly used in digital displays.
CMYK
A color model using cyan, magenta, yellow, and black, used in printing.
Color Psychology
The study of how colors affect human emotions and behavior.
